About Birds Today

Birds Today shows the birds you're most likely to encounter at your location this week, laid out as a living field guide — the more likely a bird, the bigger it's drawn.

How it works

  • Your location. It uses your device's location, or a spot you choose on the map (📍). Nothing about you is stored.
  • The prediction runs in your browser. It uses the BirdNET geographic model — the same project behind BirdNET sound ID — to estimate, for the current week of the year, how likely each of ~6,000 species is to occur at that spot. The model runs entirely on your device; no location is sent to any server, and it works offline after the first load.
  • Residents shows the species present now, each sized by how likely you are to see it this week.
  • Migration shows only species arriving now — whose chance of occurrence is rising sharply this week — sized by how fast they're increasing.
  • Hover a bird for its name and how often it's seen here this week; click it for photos and sounds on the Macaulay Library. Use 👍 / 👎 to rate an illustration.

Where the images come from

  • Most are hand-coloured plates from two public-domain works, via the Internet Archive: John Gould — The Birds of Europe (1837) and H. E. Dresser — A History of the Birds of Europe (1871–1881).
  • The Gould / Dresser / AI switch chooses the source. Species without a historical plate fall back to an AI-generated field-guide illustration, drawn from a reference photograph and the species' key identifying features.
